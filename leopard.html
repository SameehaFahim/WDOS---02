One of the park's most iconic residents is the Sri Lankan Leopard, a species known for its elusive nature and striking beauty. Yala boasts a significant leopard population, providing visitors with a unique opportunity to witness these majestic big cats in their natural habitat. The coexistence of leopards with other wildlife species in Yala National Park creates a captivating and dynamic ecosystem, making it a must-visit destination for those seeking an unforgettable wildlife experience in Sri Lanka.

The Sri Lankan Leopard, a captivating and elusive predator, thrives in the grassy plains and dense forests of Horton Plains. With a larger and broader facial structure than its lowland counterparts, these leopards have adapted to the cold weather and abundant high-quality protein sources, primarily in the form of Sambhur, the largest deer species in Sri Lanka. Despite their impressive size, these leopards expertly navigate the tall grass, remaining concealed as they stalk prey or venture into lower elevations seeking easy livestock. Horton Plains serves as a critical habitat for these majestic big cats, adding to the park's allure for wildlife enthusiasts and conservationists alike.

Regarding leopards, Kumana National Park is home to these elusive predators amidst its diverse habitats. While leopards are not as prominently observed as the birdlife, lucky visitors may catch a glimpse of these magnificent cats prowling through the park's wilderness. The coexistence of leopards within the park adds to the overall biodiversity and contributes to Kumana's status as a unique and varied natural reserve in Sri Lanka.

Among its notable inhabitants is the Sri Lankan Leopard, a majestic and elusive big cat. The leopard population in Wilpattu adds to the park's allure, providing visitors with the opportunity to witness these captivating creatures in their natural habitat. The park's diverse landscapes and the presence of leopards contribute to Wilpattu's status as a vital conservation area and a captivating destination for those seeking a glimpse of Sri Lanka's rich biodiversity.
